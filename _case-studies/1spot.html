Area of Focus

Mobility for the blind or visually impaired (BVI)

I was interested in exploring accessibility focused mobility solutions, specifically for those who needed information to be delivered in alternative modalities (visually, editorially, tactically, etc.). We sought to explore ways to make information necessary for transportation (walking, busing, ride sharing) available in modalities other than sight.

Proposed Solution

Spot, an app that provides accessible walking routes, and essential navigation information typically not available to those with sensory impairments, in a form that fits their needs.

Why an App?

We chose to deliver this through an app for two reasons:

  1. Our users wanted to feel normal and not stand out--just like we all do--so they prefer everyday technology
  2. Most mobile devices come with assistive technologies already built in. These include: screen readers, dictation tools, and haptic feedback. These make phones and smart watches very accessible. Blind and visually impaired communities already heavily utilize technology, so these devices are already present in their lives.

Insights

Key Findings

  • Many visually impaired individuals are very mobile
  • Accessible designs are often only in one modality
  • Biggest barrier is a lack of access to information (in a modality suited to them)
  • Community wants the resources to do things on their own, i.e., independence

Key Problems

  • Much of the technologies, infrastructures and services the blind and visually impaired use and navigate with are not designed with accessibility in mind
  • The most easily and financially accessible strategies do not convey rich environmental information. So a user might know what is immediately around them, but not much beyond that if they are not familiar with the area
    • New and developing technologies that do provide environmental information are often expensive



Demographics of San Diego's BVI population

Mobility and Visual Impairment in San Diego

  • 6-10% of San Diego's community is disabled [1, 2, 3]
  • The blind and visually impaired make up 20% of that population. Physical or mobile disabilities make up another 50%
  • These numbers do not consider the growing aging population
  • The blind and visually impaired have a 65-70% unemployment rate

Preexisting mobility strategies, tools & resources

Current Strategies

  • Our blind and visually impaired community collaborators identified walking as a primary mode of travel, along with public transportation
    • It is relied heavily on, especially as a way to facilitate other modes of transportation (e.g. buses, trains, medical transportation)
    • Typically, walking is only done in places that the BVI individual is familiar enough with to possess a mental maps, which they can use to navigate
  • White cane users are able to detect obstacles in their path. Whereas guide dog users are led around obstacles and are not made aware of them. Each tool produces a different understanding of the user's immediate environment
  • Most other information is attained through GPS and applications that provide directions, or technologies designed to interpret the environment for the user through a variety of means [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]
  • Research has identified barriers (e.g. poorly maintained sidewalks, obstructions, lack of pathways) in the pedestrian environment to walking as a major issue, and our collaborators also expressed a desire for increased walkability (Harkey, et al., 2007).

Current Tools & Resources

For each tool I compared/looked at a number of factors, including: the quality of information, presence of information on pedestrian barriers, accessible design & ease of use, cost, availability, integration with public transit, if the information delivery method was discreet and the tool enabled independence.
No tool fully addressed all areas of concern. Those that came close lacked crucial information on pedestrian accessibility and environmental barriers, while those that provided it were limited to short sessions of guidance from another person.

Out of a dozen the 4 that most critera and/or were already used by the community were Google Maps, Project Sidewalk, Aira, and Be My Eyes.

  • Google Maps and Project Sidewalk collected environmental information that was constantly available to users via an app or web browser, and free to use. The richness of the environmental information varied in quality for both, and required the user to find the exact information they needed in a large. Google Maps provided the most comprehensive information in terms of navigation, travel, and public transit, but was missing some crucial accessibility information (state of sidewalks, presence of ramps or truncated dome mats, obstacles or parking lots, etc.). While Project Sidewalk used "crowdsourcing, computer vision, and online map imagery" to create a web based map that was specifically focused on the accessible navigability of the environment (presence of curb ramps, surface problems, obstacles, and lack of sidewalks), but limited to a small community.
  • Aira and Be My Eyes connected BVI users with an agent to help them do a multitude of tasks, including navigating their environments, finding out or reading public transit schedules, finding taxis or ride shares, in real time. Both were limited to the duration of a phone or video call, and required working with a sighted individual to provide environmental information. Be My Eyes was free, but it required the user to point their phone camera at the environment or object, while Aira started at $300 but was more discreet through the use of Google Glasses and potentially headphones.

Painpoints/ Issues/ problems/ challenges identified

  • Unfamiliar places are difficult to orient in and navigate, as such BVI individuals do not often travel to new places. This greatly increases the difficulty of completing tasks essential to everyday life or one’s health, such as going to a doctor’s appointment, finding and maintaining social support, shopping for groceries, as well finding and maintaining employment.
    This hurts not only the individual but the city they reside in, as the capital they could generate and spend is significantly reduced. Those in the BVI community often have very small social networks and support because of transportation challenges, decreasing mental and emotional wellbeing (Bascom, 2017) – another source of lost capital. In addition, insights or innovations BVI individuals could bring to spaces/areas such as businesses, academia, or the art world are greatly hindered or lost.

As one interviewee stated: "The inability to drive these days effects everything from employment to the ability to do basic things to provide for ourselves (from visiting doctors to purchasing groceries). I've often said that the only reason I would want sight is so I could drive. I don't care about the rest."
"nobody is designing walkable cities anymore."

  • Understandably, places demand pre-planning. The information needed to do so for walking includes: the availability of sidewalks, what a street crossing is like, if there are crosswalks and accessible technology along the route, the presence of parking lots, and unexpected events, like construction, that may close off sidewalks. None of this information is readily available to those who might need it?/ users, and even when they are in the environment, they often have no way of getting this information unless they ask others around them (which is burdensome, and many do not want to do).


In summary, access to environmental information allows the blind and visually impaired to travel safely and independently, especially in unfamiliar areas. Tools that are discreet and facilitate independence are preferred.

Is there need for this project? / Business Case Potential Impact / Why Spot

  • Making this information available is key to enabling individuals to remain independent and mobile. Lack of either or both interferes with the ability to find or maintain a job, fulfill basic tasks like going grocery shopping or going to the doctors, and even maintain a network of social support. This helps people remain mobile and independent; making such essential tasks much safer and efficient.
  • But it also has far-reaching impacts for San Diego at large: more people working, visiting more local businesses, generating and spending money here. Freeing up family and friends who have had to take time out of their lives and jobs to help their loved ones
  • Most importantly, it brings more diverse individuals into spaces they did not or could not access readily before. And diversity of ideas in these spaces is what makes San Diego a leader and the unique place it is. Spot isn’t just for those who need accessible routes, or even tourists who want to know where they can walk around San Diego, Spot is for San Diego (and everyone in it).

Proposed Solution

Pitch video: https://biteable.com/watch/spot-1589551
We propose to provide this information through Spot, a navigation application? dedicated to providing accessible walking routes/ providing accessibility-focused walking directions. With Spot we can bring environmental information the user needs to them in an accessible format. It would allow routes to be planned beforehand or on the go. Most importantly, it would not replace a white cane or seeing eye dog but supplement it.
An “accessible route” means that it considers things like the presence of sidewalks or dangerous unstructured crossings like parking lots, if those spaces are actually utilized for walking, and could alert users to real-time changes, such as path obstructions from construction--all things that existing navigation technologies cannot and do not do.

How we get the environmental data

In partnership with GE, San Diego recently installed over 3,000 smart streetlamps in the downtown area. These/They collect a multitude of data streams, including traffic activity, vehicle speed, parking spaces, auditory information, weather conditions and where and when pedestrians are active.
Using pedestrian activity data, we would generate a score that ranks the walkability of an area, both historically and in real time. Scores can then be used to generate accessible routes, as well as notify users of day-to-day changes, like/such as closed sidewalks. To cover gaps in data the community would be able to provide information as well; for example, if there is a heavily walked route, but it is not on a sidewalk.
